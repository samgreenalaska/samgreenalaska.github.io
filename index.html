Why Alaska oil & gas

Built for remote, harsh, high-consequence work

My career started in Alaska oil and gas, with field engineering on the Kenai Peninsula, and grew into the highest-consequence work in the industry: deepwater subsea intervention in the Gulf of Mexico. The environments differ, but the discipline is the same one Alaska operations demand: meticulous planning, ownership of execution, and reliability when conditions are unforgiving and help is far away. I'm bringing that full arc back home to Alaska oil and gas, from the North Slope and Cook Inlet to remote fields and Anchorage-area engineering offices.

Execution in unforgiving environments

I lead operations on vessels and platforms where weather, pressure, and isolation leave no margin, the same constraints that define Alaska field work on the Slope, in Cook Inlet, and at remote sites.

Ownership & logistics

End-to-end planning, equipment readiness, scheduling, and cross-discipline coordination: getting the right people, tools, and procedures to a remote worksite on time.

HSE discipline

Risk assessments, JSAs, process hazard analyses, and Stop Work Authority. I work to API 6A / 17D and regulatory standards, and I lead safety reviews on the floor.

Alaska connection: I came up in this industry in Alaska. I hold a Petroleum Engineering degree from the University of Alaska Fairbanks, ran my field engineering internships with Hilcorp Alaska in Kenai, and call Big Lake home. For Alaska employers that means someone who already knows the state, the work, and the demands of remote operations, and who is committed to building a career here. I'm ready for a rotation on the Slope or at a remote field, and Big Lake puts me a short commute from engineering offices in Wasilla, Palmer, Eagle River, and Anchorage.
